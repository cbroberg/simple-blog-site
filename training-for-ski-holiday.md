A ski holiday is one of the year's greatest experiences – but far too many people arrive in the mountains without properly preparing their bodies. The result? Sore muscles by day two, fatigue midday, and an experience that never reaches its full potential.

With the right preparation, however, you can enjoy every single day on the slopes – from the first run in the morning to the last turn in the evening sun.

---

## Why Is Fitness So Important for Skiing?

Skiing is far more physically demanding than most people realise. Even though you're not running or jumping, your body is constantly working to maintain balance, absorb shocks, and control movements at high speed.

The primary muscles used during skiing are:

- **Quadriceps (thighs)** – worked intensively during turns and braking
- **Hamstrings and glutes** – stabilise the body in all movements
- **Calves and ankles** – keep you steady in your boots
- **Core (abs and back)** – maintain balance and protect your spine

If these muscles aren't trained, they'll give out far sooner than you'd like. That means short days on the slopes, aching muscles, and an increased risk of injury.

## The Difference Between a Fit and Unfit Skier

![Skier in black gear by a ski sign in Mayrhofen with snow-covered mountains in the background](/uploads/og/skiferie-mayrhofen-oestrig.jpg "float:left|width:40%")

The difference is noticeable – and it shows up on day one:

**The unfit skier:**

- Feels fatigue in the thighs after 3–4 runs
- Struggles to maintain technique when muscles are tired
- Risks falling and getting injured because the body doesn't react fast enough
- Spends the best hours in the lodge recovering for the next day

**The fit skier:**

- Can maintain energy and technique all day long
- Has better balance and reacts faster in unexpected situations
- Takes on the difficult runs with confidence and joy
- Ends the day with a smile

The difference isn't just physical – it's also about getting more value out of your holiday. A ski holiday is expensive. Getting the most out of every single day starts at home, months before departure.

## The Best Exercises to Prepare for Skiing

You don't need a gym membership or advanced equipment. The following exercises are effective and can be done at home:

### 1. Squats and Wall Sit

Build strength in the thighs and glutes – the exact muscles you'll rely on in every turn.

- **Squat:** 3 sets of 15 reps
- **Wall sit:** Hold for 60 seconds, repeat 3 times

### 2. Lunges

Train leg stability and balance – essential for skiing on uneven terrain.

- **Forward lunges:** 3 sets of 12 reps per leg
- **Side lunges:** 3 sets of 10 reps per leg

### 3. Jump Training (Plyometrics)

Boost your explosive power and the ability to change direction quickly.

- **Jump squats:** 3 sets of 10 reps
- **Lateral jumps:** 3 sets of 15 reps

### 4. Balance and Stability

Practise standing on one leg, use a balance board, or try yoga poses.

- **Single-leg stand:** 3 × 45 seconds per leg
- **Balance board:** 5 minutes daily

### 5. Cardio and Endurance

Skiing demands cardiovascular endurance. Build it with:

- Running, cycling, or rowing
- Interval training (HIIT) – 20–30 minutes, 3 times a week

### 6. Core Training

A strong core protects your back and keeps you upright in challenging situations.

- **Plank:** 3 × 45 seconds
- **Russian twist:** 3 sets of 20 reps
- **Glute bridge:** 3 sets of 15 reps

## An 8-Week Training Plan

| Week | Focus | Frequency |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1–2 | Building base strength – squats, lunges, core | 3 × per week |
| 3–4 | Increase intensity – add jump training and balance | 4 × per week |
| 5–6 | Endurance and cardio – intervals + strength | 4–5 × per week |
| 7–8 | Ski-specific training – combine all elements | 5 × per week |

Start at least 8 weeks before your trip for the best results. Even 4 weeks of consistent training makes a significant difference.
